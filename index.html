01 — THE SCIENCE

Why Does It Actually Fly?

A paper plane is a surprisingly faithful model of real aircraft aerodynamics. Four invisible forces are constantly at war whenever it takes to the air.

↑

Lift

Air moving over the curved upper surface of a wing travels faster than air beneath it. According to Bernoulli's principle, faster air has lower pressure — so the wing gets pushed upward. The angle the plane makes with oncoming air (called the angle of attack) also deflects air downward, creating an equal upward reaction force.

P + ½ρv² = constant
Bernoulli's Principle
←

Drag

Every surface facing the airflow resists motion. Paper planes suffer from form drag (their blunt cross-section) and induced drag (the turbulence shed off wingtips). A sleeker fuselage, swept wings, and a pointed nose all help reduce drag — exactly what champion distance folders do.

Fd = ½ρv²CdA
Fluid Resistance
→

Thrust

Unlike a jet, your arm is the only engine a paper plane gets. The energy of your throw converts to kinetic energy — forward velocity — which gives the wings the airspeed they need to generate lift. A smooth, level release maximises usable thrust and minimises spin-induced instability.

KE = ½mv²
Kinetic Energy
↓

Gravity

Weight always acts downward through the plane's centre of gravity. For stable flight the CoG must sit slightly ahead of the centre of pressure (where lift acts). If CoG is too far back, the nose pitches up, speed bleeds off, and the plane stalls. Too far forward and it dives — a nose-heavy design every folder knows too well.

W = mg
Centre of Gravity
02 — STABILITY

Balance Is Everything

A plane that wobbles, rolls, or spins is fighting itself. Designers tune three axes of rotation to achieve stable glide:

  • Pitch — nose up / nose down. Controlled by the horizontal tail (elevator). Adding a small upward flick to the trailing edge of each wing increases the effective elevator area and pushes the nose down to recover from a stall.
  • Roll — one wing dips. Controlled by the dihedral angle — the slight V-shape of the wings seen head-on. Dihedral creates a natural self-correcting roll force: if one wing dips, the fuselage slides toward that side, increasing its lift and restoring level flight.
  • Yaw — nose swings left or right. Controlled by the vertical fin. Even a small crease along the fuselage top acts as a rudder, keeping the nose pointed into the relative wind.
03 — DESIGNS

Form Follows Flight

Different fold geometries optimise for different flight characteristics. Here are three archetypes and the physics behind each.

The Dart

Best for Distance

Long and narrow with a pronounced taper. Low wing area = low drag. The trade-off is a small lift surface, so it needs a firm throw. The CoG sits far forward due to the thick nose layers, giving reliable pitch-down tendency and predictable recovery.

Low DragHigh SpeedNose-heavy

The Glider

Best for Hang Time

Wide wingspan relative to fuselage length gives a high aspect ratio. More lift per unit speed means a gentle sink rate. Dihedral is usually built into the fold so wings angle upward slightly, maximising passive roll stability without any adjustments.

High LiftSlow SinkStable Roll

The Delta

Best for Stunts & Loops

Heavily swept delta wings push the CoG right to the neutral-stability boundary. Small adjustments to the trailing-edge angle shift the CoP forward or back, making it highly responsive. Upward flaps create spectacular loops; asymmetric flaps induce tight spirals.

Swept WingManeuverableFlap-sensitive
04 — THE RECORD
69.14m

The World Record Throw

On December 2, 2022, Dillon Ruble launched a paper plane a staggering 69.14 metres (226 ft 10 in) in Las Vegas — the farthest paper plane throw ever recorded by Guinness World Records. The design, engineered by aerodynamicist John Collins ("The Paper Airplane Guy"), used a specially tuned dart with a reinforced leading edge. Collins spent over a year refining the fold angle to just 0.3° more than a standard dart, shifting the CoG forward enough to sustain maximum glide ratio at release speed.
